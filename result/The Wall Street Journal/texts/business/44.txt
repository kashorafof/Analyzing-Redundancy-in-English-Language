A new U.S. law meant to penalize forced labor in China’s western Xinjiang region is showcasing the still-fearsome reach of American economic power—and its limits.
The Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act, which was signed into law in December and came into effect last week, requires U.S. importers to show that items from Xinjiang weren’t produced with forced labor—a difficult standard, given that many Western auditing companies have stopped investigating forced labor claims there and that the government routinely restricts outsiders’ access to sites. Western researchers say that Xinjiang authorities have imprisoned hundreds of thousands of Uyghurs, a Turkic Muslim minority, and that many have been subjected to forced labor among other abuses. Beijing denies all such claims.
